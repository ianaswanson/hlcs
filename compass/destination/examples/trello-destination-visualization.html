Trello Compass Destination Canvas - 2011

VISION

Work should be visible. When you can see everything laid out in front of you - what's done, what's happening, what's next - collaboration stops feeling like coordination overhead and starts feeling like flow. We believe the simple act of moving a card from 'Doing' to 'Done' should feel satisfying, that teams deserve tools that fade into the background and let the work shine through. We're building the whiteboard that travels with you, the sticky note that never falls off, the shared brain for any team doing anything.

PROBLEM

What's the core problem?
Teams can't see their work. Project status lives in people's heads, buried in spreadsheets, scattered across email threads, or stuck on whiteboards that don't travel. This invisibility creates constant 'what's the status?' interruptions, duplicated effort, dropped balls, and a persistent low-grade anxiety about whether things are actually getting done.

How do they solve it today?
• Spreadsheets - flexible but not visual, hard to collaborate, get stale
• Physical whiteboards and sticky notes - visual but don't travel, can't share remotely
• Basecamp - comprehensive but heavyweight, learning curve, opinionated workflow
• Email threads - ubiquitous but chaotic, no status visibility, things get lost
• Nothing - keep it all in your head, ask people constantly

Why existing solutions fail:
Existing tools force a choice: either powerful-but-complex (Basecamp, MS Project) or simple-but-limited (spreadsheets, sticky notes). The powerful tools require commitment that small teams won't make. The simple tools lack collaboration and real-time visibility. Nothing sits in the sweet spot of 'powerful enough AND instantly usable.'

Is this big enough to matter?
Basecamp has paying customers proving teams will pay for collaboration tools. But adoption is limited by complexity - most teams try these tools and abandon them. The gap between 'people who need to track work' and 'people using work-tracking tools' is massive. We're not competing for Basecamp's customers; we're expanding the market to everyone who gave up on project management software.

SOLUTION

What we're building:
Web-based kanban boards for any kind of work

Key capabilities:
• Create unlimited boards, lists, and cards
• Drag-and-drop cards between lists
• Real-time collaboration - see changes as they happen
• Comments and attachments on cards
• Works in any browser, no installation
• Free to use (core features)

CUSTOMER

Buyer: Team lead, startup founder, or department head who controls tool decisions

User: Individual contributors and team members doing the actual work

Champion: The productivity enthusiast who discovers Trello and introduces it to their team

Key characteristics:
• Tech-comfortable but not necessarily developers
• Frustrated with current tools being too rigid or too complex
• Work in small teams (2-15 people)
• Value speed and flexibility over comprehensive features

UNIQUE VALUE PROPOSITION

Why choose us?
Instantly usable. No training required. No setup wizard. No workflow configuration. You see a board, you understand it. You can teach someone Trello in 30 seconds by showing them your screen. This isn't a feature - it's the entire product philosophy. Every feature request gets evaluated against 'does this make Trello harder to understand in the first 30 seconds?'

What's our moat?
Simplicity is the hardest thing to copy. Every competitor will be tempted to add features. Product managers justify their existence by shipping features. Sales teams want checkboxes for RFP comparisons. Our moat is the discipline to say no - to keep Trello simple while others make their products complex. This is a cultural moat, not a technical one.

TIMING

Why now?
The browser just became powerful enough. HTML5, WebSockets, and modern JavaScript frameworks make it possible to build rich, real-time applications that feel native. Two years ago, the drag-and-drop experience we need wasn't possible in a browser. Two years from now, everyone will be building this. Right now, we can be first.

Market factors:
• Technology: Browser capabilities (drag-and-drop, real-time sync) just matured
• Behavioral: Startup boom creating thousands of small teams needing lightweight tools
• Market: Freemium model proven by Dropbox, Evernote
• Competitive gap: Basecamp proved the market but remains complex
• Distribution: Joel Spolsky's audience provides launch platform

MARKET

Beachhead:
Small tech teams and startups. They're comfortable with new web tools, frustrated with existing options, have urgency to move fast, and will spread the word to other teams.

Landscape:
• Basecamp - established, respected, but heavyweight
• Asana - emerging, task-focused, less visual
• Spreadsheets - ubiquitous but not purpose-built
• Physical tools - visual but not digital
• Vertical tools (Jira) - powerful but developer-specific

Size (TAM):
Beachhead: ~500K small tech teams globally. If 10% adopt and 5% convert to paid, that's $3M ARR from beachhead alone before expansion.

REVENUE MODEL

Pricing model: Freemium. Core product free forever. Paid tiers for power features: integrations, larger attachments, admin controls, priority support.

Price point: TBD - launching free, will introduce paid tiers based on usage patterns. Expecting $5-10/user/month for 'Pro' tier.

Cost drivers: Hosting/infrastructure, development team (4-6 engineers), minimal support

Unit economics: Hypothesis: free users cost ~$0.50/month to serve, paid users worth $50+ LTV, need 10% conversion to be viable.
